Background

What Mira Studio is — and who already has it

Mira Studio is Meltwater's AI-powered research and analysis workspace. Customers ask a question in plain language, and Mira scans Meltwater's news and social data to generate a structured response — key takeaways, sentiment, data points, and source links — instead of them building a search or pulling together a report by hand.

The one-liner: "From question to clarity, fast." No Boolean searches, no dashboards, no deep Meltwater expertise required — describe what you want to know, and Mira finds it.

Mira Studio is not sold as an add-on, and it's not available on legacy packages. If a customer on one of the six qualifying packages above doesn't see it in their left-hand nav, that's a provisioning issue worth flagging to Support — not a packaging question. If a customer is on Media Intelligence Essentials or a legacy package, they don't have it, full stop — there's no upsell path through this guide; loop in the Account Manager if the customer expresses interest.
Mira Studio vs. Mira API — quick distinction: Mira Studio is the in-platform UI most of your customers already have. Mira API is a separate, narrow, paid add-on that lets developers call the same underlying engine programmatically — very few customers have it. If a customer asks about pulling Mira insights into their own tools via API, that's a different conversation (see the Mira API guide on this hub) — don't conflate the two.

Why this matters

The problem Mira Studio actually solves

Most customers aren't asking for an AI feature. They're dealing with shrinking teams, rising pressure to show ROI, and too much manual work between "I have the data" and "I have a report leadership can use." Lead with that, not with the AI angle.

Before the call

Pre-session setup

The single biggest lever for a Mira Studio session that lands: show up with a Project already built and real output ready to react to. A blank prompt box and a customer who's never written one is a slow, awkward start. A pre-built Project with a relevant answer already pulled is an immediate "oh, that's useful."

The core idea — Projects first, prompts second. A Project stores everything that shouldn't change between prompts: the customer's brand, their key competitors, region, language, preferred filters, and relevant saved searches. Once that's set, prompts can be short — "what were this week's most impactful stories?" — because Mira already knows who "we" and "our competitors" means. Skip the Project, and every prompt has to restate all of that context manually, which makes prompts long, inconsistent, and more likely to get misinterpreted.
⚠️ Without a Project
"As [Brand]'s comms lead, I want to understand the most impactful coverage and social discussion related to our Smart Bricks product line and our announcements at CES this year, specifically in the US and UK markets, looking at sentiment, key themes, and how this compares to [Competitor 1] and [Competitor 2]..."
~1,340 characters — and the customer has to remember and retype all of this every time.
✅ With a Project
"What were the most impactful coverage highlights related to our CES announcements?"
~115 characters — brand, product, competitors, and region are already loaded from the Project.
Why shorter prompts actually perform better — not just easier to type: every extra clause in a long prompt is another assumption the model has to interpret, and another way the wording could land differently next time. A Project absorbs that complexity once, in a place you can inspect and reuse, instead of re-introducing it — and re-risking it — in every single prompt.
1

Build a Project for the customer before the session

Set their brand, primary product or focus area, 3–5 key competitors, region, and language. Add any Explore saved searches that are clearly relevant — these load into the prompt as a reference, not as an automatic filter, so the customer can still choose when to apply them.

💡 If you're unsure who their real competitors are, use the names confirmed in Salesforce or pre-sales notes. Two or three well-chosen names beat five guessed ones — the customer can always adjust live.
2

Set a preferred filter set if one makes sense

If the customer clearly favors certain sources, languages, or date ranges, bake that into the Project so it applies to every response by default — one less thing they need to specify per prompt.

3

Run 1–2 prompts ahead of time so there's a real answer to react to

Use the Prompt Library or a Core Prompt close to what you expect the customer cares about — something tied to their brand, a recent announcement, or a competitor comparison. Walking into a session with an actual Mira response on screen is far more convincing than describing what it could do.

🗂️ Reverse-engineer from the output: decide what a great answer looks like first, then work backward to what needs to live in the Project, and only then to the exact prompt you'd type.
4

Know where it lives and how to introduce it

Mira Studio sits in the left-hand navigation. If the customer is also new to Explore, introduce Mira Studio after Explore — once they understand saved searches, the "# to reference a search" shortcut and the underlying data model click faster.

Find their use case

Discovery questions

Mira Studio's value depends entirely on whether it's pointed at something the customer actually cares about. These four questions — asked before or early in the session — surface what that is, and tell you which persona talk track to lean into next.

1
What information are you expected to deliver regularly to your team or leadership — and why does it matter to them? Listen for: recurring reports, briefings, or updates. That recurring deliverable is your first candidate for a saved Project and a repeatable prompt.
2
What kinds of questions or requests do you frequently get from colleagues — and how do you usually answer them today? Listen for: "I pull a report," "I check a few sources and summarize," "I just know it off the top of my head." Manual synthesis work is exactly what Mira replaces.
3
Which of those recurring questions or requests do you wish you could answer faster or more easily? Listen for: visible frustration or hesitation. That friction point is the strongest entry point for a live demo — it's already a felt need, not a hypothetical one.
4
What routine tasks take longer than they should — and what's actually slowing them down? Listen for: time spent compiling, formatting, or cross-referencing rather than analyzing. That's the gap between "I have the data" and "I have something I can send" — precisely where Mira Studio sits.
What you're really doing: matching Mira Studio to a felt need, not pitching a feature. A customer who hears "this can summarize sentiment" nods politely. A customer who hears "this is the thing that would've saved you the two hours you just described" leans in.

Show the product

Product walkthrough

Walk through these in sequence. Lead with whatever Project and prompt you pre-built — that's your proof point — then show the customer how to do it themselves.

Start here Your pre-built Project & response

Show the Project you set up before the call — their brand, competitors, and filters already loaded — and the response you generated from it. Point out that this took one short prompt because the context was already there.

Key message: "This is what it looks like once it's set up for you. Let's look at how you'd ask your own questions the same way."

Three ways in Prompt Library, Core Prompts, custom prompts

There are three ways to interact with Mira Studio, and new users should start with whichever feels lowest-effort:

Prompt Library — a curated set of pre-written prompts across nine categories (Brand Monitoring, Competitive Intelligence, Industry Trends, Crisis Management, Stakeholder Briefing, Media Relations, Analysis/Reporting, Social Influencers, Social Intelligence). Recommended for anyone new to writing prompts.

Core Prompts — featured shortcuts on the Mira Studio home screen for the most common tasks: Media Brief, Measure Press Release Impact, Industry Brief, Trending News, Compare Competitor Coverage, Consumer Reactions, Social Performance Report, Identify Journalists. Clicking one loads an editable template.

Custom prompts — typed directly into the "Ask me a question…" box, for anything specific that doesn't match a library entry.

💡 Show all three, but anchor the customer to whichever matches what came up in discovery. A customer who described a recurring weekly recap should see a Core Prompt or Library entry close to that, not a blank box.
Context shortcut The # search reference

Typing # in the prompt box opens a list of the customer's existing Explore saved searches. Selecting one scopes the response to that search's results, rather than Mira's general data — useful when they already have a precise search built and want Mira to summarize or analyze just that.

💡 This is a great moment to connect Mira Studio back to Explore for customers who are already comfortable building searches — it shows the two tools working together rather than as separate, disconnected tabs.
Output Canvas View, sharing, and alerts

Every response includes key takeaways, sentiment, data points, and verified source links, with Suggested Follow-Ups at the end to keep the thread going. Canvas View opens the response in a slide-out panel for annotating, editing, and exporting.

What to show: from Canvas View, a response can be shared as a link (no Meltwater account needed to view it), downloaded as a PDF, emailed to up to 10 recipients, added as a citation to a Newsletter, or turned directly into an Alert.

💡 For customers managing stakeholders who don't log into Meltwater, the shareable link is the single most useful feature to demonstrate live — it turns Mira Studio into something they can distribute, not just something they personally use.
Beyond the basics Agents, scheduling, and branded exports

Once a customer is comfortable with core prompting, a few more advanced capabilities are worth mentioning as "what's next" rather than leading with them on a first session:

Agents (Industry Roundup, News Briefing, Coverage Report) run recurring, templated analysis automatically rather than requiring a fresh prompt each time. Scheduled Prompts let a favorite prompt re-run on a cadence — useful for the same weekly recap customers often describe in discovery. Branded PowerPoint and Web Report exports turn a Mira response into a polished, on-brand deliverable without manual formatting.

💡 These land best as a second-session topic — "now that you've seen how this works, here's how to put it on autopilot" — rather than competing for attention in an introductory walkthrough.
Where Mira Studio isn't the right tool — set expectations early. It's strongest for qualitative, conversational analysis, not as a replacement for purpose-built reporting tools.
Good fit
  • Explaining coverage or sentiment in plain language
  • Comparing brand performance to competitors
  • Spotting emerging trends or early risk signals
  • Drafting executive briefs and stakeholder summaries
  • Brainstorming copy or angles for an announcement
Point them elsewhere
  • Building a Boolean search → Explore Search Assistant
  • Quantitative, metrics-heavy reporting → Unified Dashboards
  • Journalist research and outreach lists → Media Relations
  • An exhaustive list of every mention → Explore
Closing the session

Build a prompt together, then set a cadence

Don't end a session having only shown Mira Studio — end it with the customer having used it. Then leave with a concrete next step on both sides.

Talk track — closing with a collaborative prompt: "Now that you've seen this in action — what's a question you've actually been wanting an answer to about your brand or your space? Let's build that prompt together right now, using the Project we set up, and see what comes back."
Mention the mobile app. Mira Studio is also available in the Meltwater mobile app — same Projects, same prompting. It's worth a quick mention as you wrap the session: "you don't have to be at your desk to try this between now and when we talk again — it's in the mobile app too." A customer who tries one prompt on their phone over the next week shows up far more engaged at the follow-up than one who only saw it once on a screen-share.
1

Customer: try 2–3 prompts on their own

Point them to the Prompt Library and encourage experimentation — on desktop or the mobile app — since prompts can be edited or deleted freely, so there's no risk in trying different angles before the next check-in.

2

Customer: review results in about a week

Ask them to revisit their Project and prompt history and note anything surprising, useful, or confusing — that becomes the agenda for the follow-up.

3

IM: follow up in 2–3 weeks

Review what they tried, refine the Project if their focus has shifted, and — if they're ready — introduce Agents or Scheduled Prompts as the next layer of adoption.
