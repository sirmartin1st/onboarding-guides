Step 1

Pre-call prep

The single biggest difference between a GAIL session that lands and one that doesn't is whether you show up with real data already in the platform. Build out a meaningful set of prompts before the call — not one, but 5 to 15. That's enough to generate real signal across multiple tabs and give the customer something worth reacting to.

Why 5–15 prompts? More prompts mean richer data across Trends, Benchmarking, Sentiment, and Sources. They're also low-risk — prompts can be deleted at any time and won't affect the customer's prompt capacity permanently. Think of your pre-built prompts as a demo layer the customer can keep, modify, or replace as they build their own.
1

Watch the demo and check release notes

Before any GAIL conversation, review Maia's demo video and the recording from Maxim Davis on the Saleshood page. Then check the latest Release Notes — new features ship frequently and you want to know what's changed.

2

Set up the customer's brand and competitors

Before adding any prompts, complete Brand Setup for the customer. Add their primary brand and at least three to five key competitors. This ensures the Benchmarking and Sentiment tabs have something meaningful to show when you get on the call — and it's the first thing most customers will want to verify is correct anyway.

💡 Confirm which competitors matter most by checking the customer's Salesforce account or any pre-sales notes. If you're unsure, use the top two or three names you know from their industry and let the customer adjust on the call.
3

Build 5–15 prompts using two resources

Use both of the following to build out a strong starting set. Aim for variety — brand-level, competitor-level, product-level, and category-level prompts will give the most complete picture across tabs.

GAIL Prompt Creator GPT — Custom GPT built specifically to generate prompt ideas tailored to a brand and industry. Input the customer's brand name and a few details about their space and it'll generate a solid starting list.

PM Prompt Spreadsheet — A library of prompt ideas organized by use case and industry, maintained by the product team. Use it as inspiration to identify prompt angles you might not have thought of. This is a reference resource — it's not tied to your specific customer's account.

⏱ Add prompts at least 1–2 hours before the call so there's real data to review. Prompts added right before the session may not have enough data populated yet.
4

Know where GAIL lives in the platform

GenAI Lens lives under the Monitor tab. Introduce it after covering Explore — customers who understand Explore will make the connection to GenAI Lens quickly, since both are monitoring tools focused on how a brand is being talked about.

Step 2

Framing it for their world

Before you show the product, establish the context. Most customers are new to this space — start by meeting them where they are on LLMs, then connect GenAI Lens to something they already care about.

If they're not familiar with LLMs — start here

"Before we look at the product, let me give you a quick frame. LLMs — large language models — are the AI behind tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Google's AI search results. They're trained on massive amounts of text, and when someone asks them a question about your brand, your industry, or your competitors, they generate an answer based on what they've learned from the internet."

"The challenge is that your brand is showing up in those answers — and until now, there's been no way to see how. GenAI Lens changes that."

If they're already familiar with LLMs — lead with the gap

"You're already using tools like ChatGPT or Gemini, so you know how much people are relying on them for research, recommendations, and decisions. What most teams don't have visibility into is: what are those models actually saying about your brand? What sources are shaping that narrative? And how does your share of AI voice compare to your competitors?"

"That's exactly what GenAI Lens is built to answer."

Connecting it to what they already own (Explore or Monitor users)

"You're already using Explore to track your brand across news and social. Think of GenAI Lens as extending that same visibility into a new channel — AI-generated conversations. The same way you monitor what journalists and social users are saying about your brand, GenAI Lens monitors what AI models are saying. It's a new layer on top of what you're already doing."

Common objections

Handling pushback before you get to the product

These come up before customers even see the walkthrough. Have these ready.

"Our audience isn't really using AI for research yet."
LLM referral traffic to the top 1,000 websites globally hit 1.1 billion visits in June 2025 alone — up 700% year-over-year. That shift is happening faster than most teams expect. The brands that get visibility into this now will be ahead of the curve when their customers catch up — and they're catching up quickly.
"I don't really understand how this is different from regular media monitoring."
Media monitoring tracks what journalists and social users publish publicly. LLMs are different — they synthesize information from across the web and generate new content, often in closed environments, without any public record. Traditional monitoring can't see inside those conversations. GenAI Lens is purpose-built for exactly that layer.
"Is this actually ready to use, or is it more of a beta?"
It's live and actively used by major brands today — including Heineken and global pharmaceutical and healthcare organizations. That said, it's a fast-moving product and new features are being added regularly. I'll walk you through what's live now and flag what's coming so you can plan around it.
Step 3

Product walkthrough

Walk the product in this sequence. Brand Setup first — it makes every subsequent tab more relevant. Then move through Trends, Benchmarking, Sentiment, and Sources. Use the Competitor-only toggle throughout to keep charts focused.

Before anything else: Ask the customer if their brand and key competitors appear in the default results. If not, that's the first thing to fix — Brand Setup makes the rest of the walkthrough land.
Start here Brand Setup mCommunity guide ↗

You've already completed Brand Setup as part of your pre-call prep — the customer's brand and competitors are in the platform before the call starts. Use this part of the session to walk them through what you've built and confirm it's right.

What to cover: Show them their brand and the competitors you added. Ask whether anything is missing or should be adjusted — a competitor they care about, a product line, a key person. Make any changes live so they see how easy it is to manage themselves going forward.

Key toggle: Demonstrate Competitor-only mode and explain what it does — it filters all charts to just their brand and the competitors they've added. Turn it on, show the difference, then explain they can toggle it off any time to see the full landscape.

💡 This is a natural moment to explain what "entities" means — brands, products, people, locations, and key phrases are all trackable. Ask if there are any products or people they'd want to add beyond just the brand name.
Tab 1 Trends mCommunity guide ↗

The Trends tab shows how often their brand, competitors, products, people, locations, and key phrases appear in LLM responses over time — based on the prompts they've set up.

What to show: Pull up the chart for their brand and walk them through what the mention volume means. A spike or dip is a good conversation starter — "what happened here that might have affected how AI talks about you?"

The key message: These aren't social mentions or news articles. This is specifically what AI models are saying when someone asks about your space. It's a fundamentally new signal.

💡 If there's limited data, use this as a reason to add more prompts — more prompts = more signal. It's also a good setup for the bulk upload conversation later.
Tab 2 Benchmarking mCommunity guide ↗

Benchmarking shows how their brand or competitor performs across models and entities — side by side. This is often the tab that generates the most customer reaction because it makes competitive gaps visible immediately.

What to show: Pull up the head-to-head comparison between their brand and a key competitor. Walk through what "share of model" means as a new KPI alongside traditional share of voice.

The key message: This is one of the only places a brand can see how they're performing in AI-generated conversations versus competitors — not just in news or social. It's a genuinely new type of visibility.

💡 If a competitor is showing up significantly more, that's an "aha" moment. Don't rush past it — let the customer sit with it and ask what they think is driving the gap.
Tab 3 Sentiment mCommunity guide ↗

The Sentiment tab breaks down how LLMs characterize the brand — what language, themes, and context are showing up as positive, negative, or neutral. The Sentiment Analysis widget surfaces the specific framing behind each rating.

What to show: Pull up the sentiment breakdown for their brand and walk through one or two specific examples. If there's negative sentiment, dig into what's driving it — is it accurate? Is it outdated? That's an actionable insight.

The key message: This isn't a vanity metric. The sentiment analysis tells you what specific claims or associations are shaping AI perception — so you know exactly what to address in your content and PR strategy.

💡 Negative sentiment tied to an old article or outdated narrative is a strong use case for the Sources tab follow-up — show how the two tabs connect.
Tab 4 Sources mCommunity guide ↗

The Sources tab surfaces the top outlets, journalists, and domains that appear in LLM responses — revealing who and what is actually shaping the AI narrative around their brand.

What to show: Walk through the top sources and identify any that are unfamiliar or surprising. Then demonstrate the drill-down: click into a domain to see which entities it's associated with, and use the Unified Profile to explore the journalist or outlet further.

The key message: Knowing which sources influence LLM outputs is the starting point for a GEO strategy. If their brand shows up negatively in AI, this tab tells them what content to address. If a competitor is outperforming them, this shows where that perception is coming from.

Action worth naming: From the Sources tab, customers can click the profile icon on a journalist and select "Send Email" to reach out directly from the platform — bridging monitoring into media relations in one step.

💡 GlobeNewswire and PR Newswire are consistently among the most LLM-influential sources. If the customer isn't using newswires, this is the conversation to have.
Tab 5 Prompt Management mCommunity guide ↗

Prompt Management is where the customer will live once they're active — it's how they create, organize, and manage all their prompts over time. Ending the walkthrough here is intentional: it sets up the transition directly into the Prompt Strategy section and the collaborative prompt-building moment.

What to show: Walk through how to create a new prompt using the prompt creation flow — the fields, the options, and what each setting controls. Then show how Topics and Labels can be used to organize prompts as their library grows.

The key message: The prompts you built before the call are a starting point. This is where they take ownership — adding prompts that reflect their specific questions, campaigns, and competitors. The more focused and specific their prompts, the more useful the data across every other tab.

💡 This is the natural lead-in to building a prompt together. After showing them the creation flow, hand it over: "Now let's build one based on what you've seen today."
Step 4

Prompt strategy

Prompts are the foundation of what GAIL shows. Better prompts = better data. Most new customers need guidance here — they default to vague inputs and then wonder why the results aren't useful.

How prompts work in GenAI Lens: Each prompt is submitted to multiple LLMs, which generate responses. GenAI Lens then analyzes those responses for brand mentions, sentiment, sources, and entities. The quality of your prompts directly determines the quality of your insights.

What makes a good vs. bad prompt

✅ Good prompts
Tell me about [Brand Name]. How do people generally perceive it, and where does it stand in the market right now? Specific, open-ended, mirrors how a consumer would actually ask.
What's the latest news or buzz around [Competitor Brand Name]? Named entity + context = richer, more trackable LLM responses.
How does [Brand]'s [Product] compare to [Competitor]'s [Product]? What are the key differences? Head-to-head prompts generate strong competitive data in Benchmarking.
What are the top [category] companies known for in terms of customer service? Category-level prompts surface how the brand ranks in a broader context.
❌ Prompts to avoid
Tell me about [Brand]. Too vague. LLMs return generic responses with no trackable signal.
Is [Brand] a good company? Binary framing limits the response and skews sentiment artificially.
What are all the things [Brand] has ever done? Overly broad. LLMs will hallucinate or give outdated historical content.
How many employees does [Brand] have? Factual queries don't generate useful narrative or sentiment data.
Your pre-built prompts set the standard. The prompts you add before the call are a model for what good looks like — good enough that the customer may not want to remove them. Use them during the walkthrough to show what a well-formed prompt generates, then use that output as the launchpad for building a new prompt together at the end of the call.

End of call · High-value moment

Build a prompt together

Near the end of the walkthrough, after you've reviewed an output together, build one new prompt collaboratively with the customer. This is the moment that converts a passive observer into an active user.

Talk track — closing the session with a collaborative prompt

"Now that you've seen what this data looks like — let me ask you: what's a question you'd actually want to ask an AI about your brand or your space? Something you'd be curious to know the answer to."

"Let's build that prompt right now, together. You tell me what you want to know, and I'll show you how to shape it so it generates the most useful output."

Why this works: Seeing a prompt they helped build — and the data it generates — makes the product feel personal and immediately useful. It also gives them a clear mental model for what to do next on their own. The customer leaves having already done the thing, not just watched it.
Step 5

Answering "so what do I do with this?"

This is the most common question after the walkthrough. Have a clear, actionable answer ready — tailored to whoever's in the room. Don't let the conversation end without connecting the data to something they can actually do next week.

📨

PR & media relations teams

The Sources tab shows exactly which journalists and outlets are influencing LLM responses. Use that to build targeted outreach lists — pitch the journalists who are already shaping the narrative. Positive coverage from high-influence sources improves AI brand visibility over time.

📰

Content & comms teams

GlobeNewswire and PR Newswire are consistently among the most LLM-influential sources. If the customer isn't using newswires or isn't getting consistent coverage on high-authority sites, that's the place to start for GEO improvement.

🤝

Influencer & Klear users

Top journalists from the Sources tab link directly to Klear profiles if the customer has access. This bridges LLM source data into influencer strategy — find the journalists shaping AI perception, then engage them as a content partner.

🗂️

Brand & strategy teams

Use Benchmarking and Sentiment data to establish a new baseline KPI: "share of model" alongside traditional share of voice. Set a quarterly reporting cadence and tie the data to brand health metrics they already track.

Don't skip this step. Customers who leave the walkthrough without a clear "next action" will go dark. Even a simple one — "pitch one journalist from the Sources tab this week" or "add five more prompts before our next check-in" — keeps momentum going.
Step 6

Next steps & ongoing cadence

Leave every GAIL session with three things agreed: what the customer is going to do before you talk again, when they're going to do it, and when you're checking back in. Simple as that.

1

Customer: build your own prompts this week

Ask the customer to create at least five prompts of their own before the follow-up call. They've already seen what good prompts look like and built one with you on the call — now they do it on their own. Point them to the Creating a Prompt guide and the LLM Visibility Monitoring Setup Playbook as self-service resources.

💡 Remind them that prompts can be deleted at any time with no lasting impact — there's no cost to experimenting. Encourage them to try different angles and see what generates the most interesting data.
2

Customer: review results in about a week

Ask them to log back in after about a week and look at what the data is showing. By then, the prompts they've added — and the ones you pre-built — will have accumulated enough signal to show real patterns. Encourage them to note any questions or surprises before the follow-up call.

📅 Set this expectation explicitly on the call: "Give it a week, log back in, and take a look at the Trends and Benchmarking tabs. See what stands out — we'll walk through it together when we talk."
3

IM: follow up in 2–3 weeks

Book the follow-up before you end the session. In that call, review the prompts the customer created, look at the data that's populated, and answer any questions that came up during their solo exploration.

This is the session where adoption either sticks or stalls. If the customer shows up having added prompts and explored the data on their own, adoption is real. If they haven't touched it, use the session to diagnose why and reset with a simpler starting point.

🎯 Goal for the second session: have the customer pull up a tab and tell you what they're seeing — without you narrating. Independent interpretation means the product has clicked for them.
